# Courtroom & Crime Scene Reconstruction for Complex Litigation

![[problem_14_img2.jpeg]]

**Summary**: Reconstructing crime scenes and disaster sites for courtroom use using LiDAR scans and forensic reports to simulate theories and present evidence.

**Sources**: [[B2B Problem Statements (AR_VR_MR).pdf]]

---

## Description
In complex litigation involving homicides, large-scale disasters, or industrial accidents, presenting spatial evidence to a jury or judge is difficult. Static photos and 2D diagrams fail to capture the physical context and dynamics of an event. Legal teams need a way to walk through a scene and test different forensic theories in a scientifically valid 3D environment.

## Spatial AI Solution
- **LiDAR Scene Capture**: Rapidly captures high-precision 3D scans of crime scenes or accident sites before they are disturbed.
- **Forensic Simulation**: Integrates forensic reports (ballistics, blood spatter, impact analysis) into the 3D model to simulate different event scenarios.
- **VR Courtroom Presentation**: Allows judges and juries to virtually "visit" the scene, providing a first-person perspective on the evidence.

## Grounding
Pakistan's legal system often struggles with evidence management and the objective presentation of facts in complex cases. Crime scene "tampering" is a common concern. A permanent, high-fidelity 3D digital record of a scene, captured immediately by forensic teams, can provide an indisputable "truth layer" that enhances the transparency and accuracy of the judicial process.

## MOAT
The moat is constructed from case-specific data, specialized physics simulation libraries for forensics, and the high threshold for courtroom acceptance. Building a track record of "admissible" 3D evidence creates a powerful barrier to entry. The more cases the platform handles, the more it becomes the standard for spatial evidence in the local legal ecosystem.

## Scoring Debate
- **Solution Quality (8)**: Greatly enhances the clarity and impact of forensic evidence in a legal setting.
- **Adoption Criteria (7)**: Requires buy-in from the legal community and judiciary, which can be slow to adopt new technologies.
- **Moat Confidence (8)**: Strong due to the specialized nature of forensic data and the difficulty of establishing legal precedent for new evidence types.
