# VR Healthcare Evidence

**Summary**: Synthesizes the academic evidence base for VR effectiveness in healthcare education, medical training, and psychological therapy — including four peer-reviewed studies with effect sizes.

**Sources**: `sustainability-16-08520-v2.pdf` (Sung et al. 2024), `s12909-024-06402-1.pdf` (Minouei et al. 2024), `ijerph-19-00464.pdf` (Vianez et al. 2022), `s12909-025-07402-5.pdf` (Hammouda et al. 2025)

**Last updated**: 2026-04-15

---

## Why the Evidence Base Matters

Clinical evidence is the primary purchasing criterion for healthcare institutions. The difference between "interesting pilot" and "approved procurement" in hospitals is peer-reviewed evidence. The four studies below provide:

1. **Statistical proof** of VR effectiveness for knowledge and skills outcomes
2. **Effect sizes** (SMD = standardized mean difference) that can be cited in grant applications and procurement proposals
3. **Boundary conditions** — where VR works and where it doesn't
4. **Longitudinal evidence** that effects persist beyond the training session

For 9D Technologies, these citations are directly usable in enterprise sales materials and investor decks.

## Study 1: VR in Healthcare Education Meta-Analysis (45 RCTs)

**Citation**: Sung, H.J. et al. (2024). *The Effectiveness of Virtual Reality in Healthcare Education: A Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials.* Sustainability, 16, 8520.

**Study design**: Systematic meta-analysis of **45 randomized controlled trials (RCTs)**; total n = **3,329 participants**

**Key findings** (effect sizes as Standardized Mean Difference, SMD):

| Outcome | SMD | p-value | Interpretation |
|---------|-----|---------|----------------|
| Knowledge acquisition | +0.28 | <0.001 | Small-to-medium positive effect |
| Skills performance | +0.23 | <0.001 | Small positive effect |
| **Skill completion time** | **-0.59** | <0.001 | **Medium negative effect** (VR learners faster) |
| **Satisfaction** | **+0.65** | <0.001 | **Medium-large positive effect** |
| **Confidence** | **+0.60** | <0.001 | **Medium-large positive effect** |

**By medical specialty**:
- Medical education: Strongest effect on knowledge (SMD +0.43)
- Nursing education: Consistent moderate effects across outcomes
- Dental education: **VR negative for skills** (SMD -0.69) — cadaver and phantom models still outperform VR for dental dexterity

**Key implication**: The evidence supports VR for **knowledge, speed, satisfaction, and confidence** — but not universally for fine motor skills. Dental is the exception; medical and nursing are strong.

## Study 2: VR for Anatomy Learning (24-Study Review)

**Citation**: Minouei, M. et al. (2024). *Virtual Reality in Anatomy Education: A Systematic Review.* BMC Medical Education, 24.

**Study design**: Systematic review of **24 studies** on VR-based anatomy education

**Key findings**:
- **83% of studies** showed increased learning outcomes when VR was used
- VR is most effective as a **supplement** to traditional anatomy teaching (cadaver dissection, textbooks), not a replacement
- Effect is strongest for **3D spatial understanding** — structures that are difficult to visualize in 2D (cardiac anatomy, neural pathways, joint articulation)
- **Side effects noted**: Headaches, dizziness, blurred vision in a minority of participants — important for session length design (recommend 15–30 minute VR sessions with breaks)

**Key implication**: VR anatomy should be positioned as "complementary" not "replacement" — this framing reduces faculty resistance and accelerates adoption. The 83% success rate is a strong headline for marketing.

## Study 3: VRET for PTSD in Veterans (11-Study Review + Focus Group)

**Citation**: Vianez, A. et al. (2022). *Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy for Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder: A Systematic Review.* International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health (IJERPH), 19, 464.

**Study design**: Systematic review of **11 studies** + focus group with PTSD specialists

**Key findings**:
- VRET (Virtual Reality Exposure Therapy) shows **positive impact on PTSD symptoms** across all 11 studies
- **Critical**: Gains are **maintained at 3-month, 6-month, and 12-month follow-up** — not just immediate post-training effects
- No significant group differences (VR vs. traditional exposure) in most studies, meaning VRET is **as effective as** traditional prolonged exposure therapy while being safer to administer
- Most effective for: military PTSD, sexual trauma, accident-related PTSD

**Requirements for effective VRET** (from focus group analysis):
1. Sense of **presence** — the feeling of "being there" in the virtual environment
2. **Dynamic scenarios** — static environments are less effective; trauma-relevant motion and sound matter
3. **Realistic feeling** — photorealism less important than behavioral realism (characters that react naturally)
4. **Multisensorial experience** — spatial audio, some haptic feedback, environmental cues (vibration for explosion proximity)

**Key implication**: VRET is clinically validated and provides a safer alternative to in-vivo exposure. The 12-month follow-up data is rare in psychological intervention research and is a strong differentiator for clinical adoption. This is directly applicable to military/defense contracts for 9D Technologies.

## Study 4: Open-Source Metaverse Platform for Medical Students (n=179)

**Citation**: Hammouda, O. et al. (2025). *Student Perceptions of an Open-Source Metaverse Platform for Medical Education Using Meta Quest 2.* BMC Medical Education, 25.

**Study design**: Prospective pre-post study; **n = 179 Tunisian medical students**; Unity3D platform on Meta Quest 2; open-source implementation

**Quantitative findings** (5-point Likert scale, pre→post):

| Measure | Pre-VR | Post-VR | Change |
|---------|--------|---------|--------|
| Anxiety about learning | 2.70 | 4.11 | **+52% reduction** |
| Memorization confidence | 4.29 | 4.78 | **+11% increase** |

**Qualitative findings** (student survey):
- **86%** found VR anatomically accurate
- **72%** found the experience realistic
- **82%** believe VR reduces learning anxiety
- **93%** support integrating VR into the curriculum
- **Only 51%** believe VR can replace physical labs (cadavers)

**Methodological note**: Open-source Unity3D + Meta Quest 2 implementation is replicable at low cost. This is evidence that a full XR medical training platform can be built on consumer hardware (Quest 2 = $299) with significant learning outcomes.

**Key implication**: The 51% who don't think VR can replace physical labs are actually the right framing — VR as supplement, not replacement, is the most defensible and adoptable position. The 93% curriculum integration support is the sales headline.

## Boundary Conditions and Caveats

1. **Fine motor skills**: VR is not proven for fine dexterity training (dental, microsurgery). Haptic feedback (FundamentalVR's approach) may narrow this gap but evidence is limited.
2. **Side effects**: 15–30 minute session length recommended; longer sessions risk headaches and dizziness in a minority of users
3. **Supplement vs. replacement**: VR works best alongside traditional training, not instead of it. Product positioning should reflect this.
4. **Publication bias**: Academic literature over-represents positive results. The true effect sizes may be somewhat smaller than reported.
5. **Generalizability**: Most studies are small (median n < 100) and conducted in controlled research settings. Real-world deployment outcomes may differ.
